CycleStay

Trade your apartment, not your savings.

Every summer, tens of thousands of students relocate for internships and face the same problem simultaneously: they need to sublet their place and find short-term housing in a new city. CycleStay uses cycle-finding matching — structurally identical to the algorithm behind kidney-exchange programs — to identify 3- and 4-way swaps no bilateral platform can find.

What you’re looking at

In production, the matcher runs once — after a 6-week submission window closes and a critical mass of student listings has accumulated. This admin then operates over the full pool to find cycles — multi-way swaps where 2–4 students trade apartments — with bilateral pairs as the fallback for anyone who doesn’t fit a cycle.

For the demo, we re-run live as you tweak parameters so you can see the engine behave under different conditions; live submissions from the marketing site are mixed into a baseline cohort to simulate the density of a real submission round.

Scenario evaluation

What this answers: "Does the matcher work the same way in different kinds of markets?"

We run the same algorithm against three test markets: Balanced (every city sends and receives equally — a toy floor), Realistic (NYC and SF dominate, like real intern flows), and Sparse (only 60 students — what happens on a thin market). The five metrics in each card tell you the match rate, how many cycles formed, and how high-quality the matches were. A platform that works only in one regime isn't really a platform.

Click Run evaluation above to run the three scenarios.

Dropout robustness

What this answers: "What happens if a student backs out at the last minute?"

We randomly remove 10% of the students who got matched, then check each affected cycle: can the matcher swap in a substitute from the unmatched pool, or does the chain collapse and force everyone in it back to Airbnb? The repair rate is the single most important number for a real launch — if it's high, the network is forgiving; if it's low, you need a deeper standby pool or a stricter commitment mechanism.

Click Run evaluation above to populate this section — dropout simulation runs against the same three scenarios.

Multi-seed benchmarks (20 seeds, error bars = 1 stdev)

What this answers: "Is the cycle lift real, or did we just get lucky on one run?"

We generate 20 different random markets (different student populations, dates, and unit mixes) and run both bilateral-only matching and cycle-finding on each. The chart shows the average match rate for each algorithm and a horizontal whisker showing how much the result varies across runs (one standard deviation). If the cycle bar sits clearly to the right of the bilateral bar with no whisker overlap, the lift holds up — it's not noise.

Click Run 20-seed benchmark above to populate. Takes ~5 seconds.

Sensitivity heatmap (match rate by skew × min-overlap)

What this answers: "Where does the matcher break down, and where does it shine?"

Most engines work well in the middle of their parameter space and fall apart at the edges. We sweep two knobs at once — market skew (how concentrated city flows are) and minimum date overlap (how strict we are about trip-date alignment) — and color each cell by the resulting match rate. Bright cardinal cells = the matcher works well; pale cells = it struggles. This is how you spot which combinations of real-world conditions you should be careful about.

Click Run sensitivity sweep above to populate. Sweeps 30 parameter combinations; takes ~3 seconds.
